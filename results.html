What has been found out: main takeaways

Average Pollutant

What is the geographical distribution of the annual NO₂ and PM2.5?

Quantile classification method allows a clear visualisation of the spatial distribution of the phenomenon. In 2022, NO₂ levels were highest in a corridor stretching from the capital region toward Ostrava, passing through Brno and Olomouc—some of the largest cities in the country. In comparison, the geography of PM2.5 pollution was more concentrated in the eastern part of the country, with lower values observed around the capital.

Yearly Concentration 2022

How did the country perform in 2022 according to the five EU air quality categories?

Based on the interpretation of EU legal limit values, the Czech Republic performed quite well in 2022 for both pollutants. NO₂ levels, in particular, fell mostly within Class 1 and 2. It is worth noting that EU standards are generally less strict than the WHO air quality guideline levels; however, continuous updates are being made in alignment with the European Green Deal objectives.

Annual Average Difference

How much does the air quality in 2022 deviate from the recent 5 years average?

As the maps tend to red, the higher the value compared to the 2017–2021 mean. On the contrary, blue areas indicate lower values, suggesting an improvement in air quality. NO2 shows a general improvement in 2022, with the exception of some areas in the capital region, Ostrava and the south-western border with Germany. PM2.5 shows a sharper improvement, displayed in the eastern part of the region and around the main urban areas. This could be related to the 2020-2021 COVID-19 pandemic and linked to a global consistent annual reduction trend. Considering nitrogen dioxide is primarily emitted by combustion processes in industry and energy supply and road traffic, the effects of the lockdown might not be that meaningful. Moreover, changes in energy use due to the war in Ukraine might have altered the use of coal or other fuels in certain areas.

Urban pollution

Time-series statistics per land cover (class 5: settlements)

Urban settlements result from the classification of the land cover layer of 2022 according to the IPCC Land Use Classification.
Without contextualisation, looking at the graphs showing the mean trend values of NO₂ and PM2.5 concentrations in urban areas might be misleading. Firstly as seen globally, from 2013 to 2018 we find non-significant changes in fine particulate matter and nitrogen dioxide, which mean values are almost stable over the years, or at least they do not show clear patterns. NO2 graph reveals a significant drop in 2019 that can be attributed to the cumulative effect of these air quality policies, as well as a possible reduction in traffic, consistent with observed trends in other European countries. In both graphs the further decrease in 2020 corresponds with the onset of the COVID-19 pandemic, when lockdowns and restrictions drastically reduced road traffic and industrial activity, leading to a sharp, temporary reduction in polluting emissions. The slight rebound in 2021 and 2022 likely reflects the gradual resumption of activities as pandemic restrictions eased.

Population Exposure

What share of the population is exposed to different levels of air pollution?

The assessment of population exposure to air pollutants in the Czech Republic for 2020 reveals a differentiated distribution across five exposure classes, with class 1 representing the lowest exposure (below 58,669 inhabitants) and class 5 the highest (up to 65,965 inhabitants). The pie charts indicate that for PM2.5, 57.1% of the population is exposed to lower pollutant classes, while 42.9% experience higher exposure levels. For NO2, the population is almost evenly split, with 50.8% in higher exposure classes and 49.2% in lower ones.
The spatial maps show that most inhabitants reside in areas with medium PM2.5 concentrations, suggesting that while extreme exposures are limited, a significant portion of the population is still subject to non-negligible pollution levels. The population class map further highlights that higher population densities tend to coincide with higher pollutant concentrations, especially in urban and industrial regions.
The bivariate maps provide additional insight by overlaying pollutant concentrations with population density. The NO2 bivariate map shows that high concentrations of NO2 are strongly associated with densely populated urban areas, indicating that city dwellers are more likely to be exposed to elevated NO2 levels. In contrast, the PM2.5 bivariate map displays a broader spatial spread, with medium to high concentrations affecting both urban and some peri-urban areas, suggesting a more diffuse source profile for PM2.5, possibly including regional transport and secondary formation processes.
